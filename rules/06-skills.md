# Skills – Displaying your knowledge
<!-- source: Sidherun PHB 2_8_2026.docx -->

Sidherun is a game which can be applied to various genres of role-playing games (RPG) and many different 'ages' within genres. In most RPGs, the 'average' person is someone who performs a function in society, displaying some sort of professional knowledge. This game measures that professional knowledge in two ways:

- Skills
- Specialties

***A Skill*** is knowledge which a character has learned during their lives, which can be used to generate some sort of outcome.

- Example(s):
  - A *Skill* in fishing may allow a villager to feed their family
  - A *Skill* in hunting may allow a person to find nearby game
  - *A Skill* in piloting may allow a person to drive a car, steer a wagon, sail a small boat

***A Specialty*** is an exceptional form of a *Skill, or a* combination of *Skills,* typically derived from sort of structured training or apprenticeship

- Example(s):
  - A *Specialty in* fishing may give a trained fisherman the ability to know where the best salmon can be found in any freshwater stream
  - A *Specialty in hunting* may give a trained huntsman the ability to find game in areas where game may not be normally available, or allow for larger game to be found
  - A *Specialty in piloting* may give a trained Reinsman the ability to drive an 8-horse carriage across dangerous terrain while being chased by robbers

## Impact of Skills

*Skills* impact the game of Sidherun and the characters who play in many ways. This impact is measured in '*Skill Bonuses*', which are applied to rolls made when a character tries *to do something.*

**How to determine if a *Skill works?***

*Skills* work off a character's base attribute for the 'thing the character is trying to do'. This determination is made during character creation, and may be based on the 'type of thing' your character wants to do, along with any story-based influences

- Example(s):
  - Ingrid is lost and by herself in the Wildes at the base of the Spine of the World mountains, deep in Vale Dwarf country. She hasn't eaten in days, and the early Spring days haven't yet revealed any game to hunt. Ingrid wants to use her herbalism skill (created during character creation) to find edible plants, but her rolls have failed her thus far. Let's see how that roll would work:
    - Ingrid has an herbalism skill based on her Intellect Attribute (16)
    - Ingrid's been able to work on her herbalism skill during her various adventures and has allocated 15 points to this skill, bringing the total herbalism score to 31 (16(Int) + 15(Skill))
    - The GM has assigned a difficulty to this check of 85 (a very hard roll for a low-level character to overcome, but appropriate to the story and the difficulty of what Ingrid is trying to do. Ingrid has rolled each day of her search:
      - Day 1, Ingrid rolled a 51, then added her combined herbalism score (31) resulting in a total skill attempt of 82, which means Ingrid failed to find any edible herbs on day one
      - Day 2, Ingrid rolled a 13, which results in a total of 44 when the scores are added. Meaning, Ingrid went hungry, yet again
      - Day 3, the GM tells Ingrid she is very frail and tired, and having trouble traveling her full distance searching, so increases her difficulty to 90, a truly terrible number to overcome. Luckily, Ingrid rolls a 77, which gives her a total of 108, beating the target and Ingrid successfully finds those edible herbs.

**When to Roll**

A roll will typically only be called for when:

- The outcome is uncertain
- Failure would meaningfully change the situation
- Success is not narratively guaranteed

If none of these are true, the action succeeds without a roll.

**Difficulty Targets**

Sidherun uses consistent difficulty targets to convey narrative scale and risk.

| Difficulty | Target | Narrative Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Effortless | | No roll required |
| Simple | 50 | Routine for trained individuals |
| Straightforward | 75 | Requires attention or care |
| Challenging | 100 | Meaningful risk, requires competence |
| Demanding | 125 | Heroic effort or favorable conditions |
| Intense | 150 | Exceptional feat |
| Rigorous | 175 | Near the edge of mortal capability |
| Vexing | 200+ | Legendary or nearly impossible |

GMs may:

- Increase the target number
- Apply situational penalties
- Or rule an action impossible without narrative change

## Some notes:

- A character who chooses a specialty must use the specialty each level, if appropriate to maintain the Specialty bonuses. This is a 'one use per level' rule, as opposed to the typical Skill use rule.
- If a character is unable to use the skills associated with a specialty during a level, that level is skipped in regard to progression
  - Example(s)
    - Martinuk the Ranger has a specialty in pathfinding, which he is unable to make use of at all during level 4. When Martinuk levels, that bonus is skipped, and will resume if the character is able to use the pathfinding skill during level 5.
